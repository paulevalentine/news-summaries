## World News: Top Stories

### 1. US–Iran Ceasefire Holds — But Only Just

A fragile two-week ceasefire between the United States and Iran, brokered by Pakistan in Islamabad, came into force earlier this week following six weeks of conflict that devastated Iran and Lebanon, drove up global fuel costs and rattled financial markets. The deal was struck hours before President Trump's deadline, during which he threatened to destroy Iran's power infrastructure. However, the truce is already under severe strain: Iran initially allowed a limited resumption of oil tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz, but halted it again citing Israeli strikes in Lebanon — which both Israel and the US insist are not covered by the agreement. Drone attacks also struck Kuwait and the UAE within hours of the ceasefire taking effect. Peace talks between Iran, the US and regional parties are continuing in Islamabad today. The ceasefire, while welcome, has provided little clarity on a permanent resolution.

### 2. Trump's Tariff War Reshapes Global Trade

One year on from his "Liberation Day" tariff announcement, President Trump's sweeping import tariffs continue to reorder global supply chains and strain diplomatic ties. The US Supreme Court struck down broad tariffs imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) in February, but Trump responded by invoking Section 122 of the Trade Act to impose an interim 10% blanket tariff. Meanwhile, tit-for-tat levies between the US and China have now exceeded 100% on both sides — the highest since the 1930s. A summit between Trump and Chinese President Xi Jinping is scheduled for May. Analysts describe the tariffs as the largest US tax increase as a share of GDP since 1993, costing the average American household approximately $1,500 this year. Separately, Trump threatened 50% tariffs on any nation supplying Iran with weapons, though legal analysts called this unenforceable given the IEEPA ruling.

### 3. Artemis II Crew Returns from the Moon

NASA's Artemis II mission concluded successfully on Friday (10 April) when the Orion capsule splashed down in the Pacific Ocean off the coast of San Diego, returning astronauts Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch and Canadian Space Agency astronaut Jeremy Hansen safely to Earth. The 10-day mission — launched on 1 April from Kennedy Space Center — marked the first time humans had travelled to the vicinity of the Moon since 1972. The crew completed a free-return lunar flyby before re-entering Earth's atmosphere. Recovery teams aboard the USS Murtha extracted the crew and flew them to Houston for post-mission medical evaluations. The mission is widely considered a success and lays the groundwork for the Artemis III crewed lunar landing, currently planned for later in the decade.

### 4. Peru Heads to the Polls

Peru holds a presidential election today, the ninth in fewer than ten years, with 35 candidates vying to lead a country gripped by political dysfunction, rising violent crime and persistent corruption. No single candidate is expected to win outright, making a June run-off likely. Voter disillusionment is high, with many Peruvians questioning whether the democratic process can break a cycle of presidents being impeached, jailed or forced to resign. International observers from the OAS are monitoring the vote. The outcome will have implications for the region, particularly around mining investment, bilateral relations with neighbouring states, and Peru's response to cross-border organised crime networks.

### 5. Syria's ISIS Detention Camps Face Uncertain Future

As the wider Middle East conflict continues, attention has returned to the fragile security situation in northeastern Syria, where the last major detention camp holding ISIS-affiliated women and children remains operational — and increasingly precarious. Aid workers and journalists describe deteriorating conditions, limited international oversight, and growing uncertainty over the camp's long-term future as local Kurdish-led administration struggles with resource constraints. Human rights organisations have renewed calls for Western governments with nationals in the camps to repatriate their citizens for trial at home. The situation is seen as a long-term risk to regional stability if left unresolved.

### 6. Immigration Settlement Rules Overhaul Moves Forward

The government's overhaul of immigration rules for indefinite leave to remain (ILR) is progressing, with the Home Office confirming that its controversial "earned settlement" framework will now be implemented in autumn 2026, after a delay from the original April start date. The reforms double the standard qualifying period from five to ten years for most work and long-stay visa routes, and introduce a minimum taxable earnings threshold of £12,750 over the qualifying period. Critics argue the changes will disproportionately affect care workers, dependent family members and lower-income migrants. A parliamentary committee has cautioned that reforms of this scale must be implemented carefully to avoid unintended consequences, noting the Home Office's track record of administrative delays.

### 7. Government Invests £15M in AI Knife Crime Mapping

The Home Office has announced a £15 million investment over three years in AI-enhanced crime mapping technology to target knife crime hotspots across England and Wales. The initiative uses precision hexagonal micro-geography to identify high-risk areas and guide preventative policing. The investment comes as official statistics show knife crime in England and Wales has fallen for a second consecutive year — down 9% in the year to September 2025 — though the government says sustained action is still required. The AI programme is part of a wider commitment to halve knife crime within a parliament. Campaigners and charities broadly welcomed the technology investment but cautioned it should complement, not replace, community-based prevention work.

### 8. MPs' Pay Rises 5% to Nearly £100,000

Members of Parliament received a 5% pay increase on 1 April, raising their basic annual salary to £98,599. The rise, recommended by the Independent Parliamentary Standards Authority (IPSA), was justified on the grounds of recruiting and retaining experienced candidates. The timing — amid a cost-of-living squeeze for many households — prompted criticism from opposition parties and trade unions, who argued that public sector workers, including nurses and teachers, continue to receive below-inflation settlements. Supporters of the increase pointed out that MPs' pay has lagged behind inflation over recent years and that the independent body sets pay at arm's length from politicians themselves.
