### 1. Iran War: Oil Prices Ease as Trump Signals Ceasefire Optimism

Oil markets pulled back on 17 April after President Trump struck an upbeat tone on prospects for a permanent settlement with Iran, with Brent crude falling toward $98 a barrel after jumping 4.7% the day before. The US–Israeli war on Iran — dubbed "Operation Epic Fury" — triggered what the International Energy Agency has called the most severe oil supply shock in history, with prices at one point surging past $120 a barrel. A ceasefire was announced on 8 April, but traffic through the Strait of Hormuz remains far below pre-war levels. Airlines worldwide are cancelling routes and reducing schedules amid surging jet fuel costs, with one European airport group warning of a systemic fuel shortage if Hormuz traffic does not normalise by month's end. The US Senate on Thursday rejected a war powers resolution that would have limited the president's ability to continue the conflict, with all Republican senators bar Rand Paul voting it down 52–47.

### 2. IMF Warns Iran Conflict Could Tip World Into Recession

The International Monetary Fund warned this week that the global economy risks tipping into recession if the energy disruption caused by the Iran war persists. In a severe scenario — where oil and gas prices spike 100–200% relative to January levels and stay elevated into 2027 — global growth could fall to just 2%, the threshold the IMF defines as near-recession. The Fund also flagged that, even if the conflict were resolved immediately, the world faces an oil shortfall for the rest of the year. The report prompted a flurry of calls from campaigners and economists for windfall taxes on oil and gas companies, which are estimated to be banking more than $30 million in extra profit every hour, with Saudi Aramco, ExxonMobil and Gazprom among the biggest beneficiaries.

Russia struck Ukrainian cities and civilian areas with hundreds of drones and dozens of cruise and ballistic missiles in a sustained overnight assault, killing at least 16 people and wounding dozens more. The attack, which stretched for several hours, targeted infrastructure and residential districts across multiple regions. Ukraine's air defences intercepted a significant number of the incoming projectiles, but the scale of the attack overwhelmed some systems. The assault drew condemnation from European leaders and renewed calls for accelerated weapons deliveries to Kyiv, as diplomatic negotiations over a peace framework remain stalled.

### 4. Israel and Lebanon Agree 10-Day Ceasefire

President Trump announced that Israel and Lebanon have agreed to a 10-day ceasefire in Israel's ongoing campaign against the Iran-backed Hezbollah militia in southern Lebanon. The deal, brokered with US involvement, is intended to create space for broader diplomatic talks and comes amid heightened regional tensions following the wider conflict with Iran. The ceasefire does not apply to Gaza, where a separate, fragile pause in fighting agreed earlier in the year has been repeatedly tested by reported violations. International observers welcomed the Lebanese agreement but cautioned that previous ceasefires in Lebanon have broken down quickly.

### 5. Turkey Reels From Two School Shootings in Two Days

Turkey is in national mourning after two school shootings in less than 48 hours. On 14 April, a former student injured 16 people at a vocational school in Siverek before taking his own life. The following day, a 14-year-old student opened fire at Ayser Çalık Secondary School in Kahramanmaras, killing nine people — including a mathematics teacher, Ayla Kara, who died attempting to shield her pupils — and wounding 13 others. The attacker used five guns and seven magazines belonging to his father, a former police officer. Until this week, school shootings were extremely rare in Turkey. Teaching unions Eğitim-İş and Eğitim-Sen announced a three-day nationwide strike beginning 15 April, while dozens of students were arrested over social media posts implying copycat attacks.

## Section 2: UK News — Domestic Overview

### 1. Mandelson Vetting Scandal: Senior Civil Servant Exits as Calls for Inquiry Grow

The political fallout from revelations about Peter Mandelson's appointment as UK Ambassador to Washington continues to escalate. It emerged that Mandelson failed security vetting in late January 2025 — with officials citing concerns including links to Jeffrey Epstein — but the Foreign Office used a rarely invoked authority to override the recommendation and clear him for the role. Prime Minister Keir Starmer has said he was unaware the security advice had been overruled. A senior Foreign Office civil servant left their post this week over the affair. Opposition parties are demanding a full inquiry and calling on Starmer to resign if it can be shown he misled parliament about the circumstances of Mandelson's appointment. Mandelson was subsequently sacked as ambassador after a US Congressional committee released documents detailing his ties to Epstein, and he was arrested in February on suspicion of misconduct in public office.

### 2. IMF Says UK Will Be Hardest-Hit G7 Economy in 2026

The International Monetary Fund's updated World Economic Outlook has singled out the UK as the worst-affected advanced economy from the Iran war's energy shock. Growth is now forecast at just 0.8% for 2026 — down from 1.3% projected last year — and the IMF's "benign" scenario puts GDP per capita growth at a mere 0.3%, the lowest in the G7. The Bank of England held interest rates at 3.75% at its March meeting, with CPI inflation running at 3.0% in January 2026, and economists warn the combination of elevated energy costs, weak trade and tight household budgets leaves the UK particularly exposed to any further deterioration in the Strait of Hormuz situation.

### 3. Southport Inquiry Finds "Catastrophic Failures" by Multiple Agencies

The Phase 1 report of the Southport public inquiry, published on 13 April, concluded that the July 2024 knife attack at a children's Taylor Swift dance event — in which three girls were killed — was foreseeable and avoidable. The report found "catastrophic failures" across every agency involved, including police, health services and social care, with no organisation taking responsibility for assessing and managing the risk posed by the perpetrator despite his violent behaviour being "clearly, repeatedly, and unambiguously signposted over many years". The report identified a systemic "merry-go-round" of state bodies refusing to act, and made 67 recommendations. Ministers pledged urgent reform, but victims' families demanded accountability rather than promises. Phase 2 of the inquiry — examining knife laws, online radicalisation and multi-agency risk management — is expected to report in spring 2027.

### 4. Pressure Mounts on Government Over Oil Windfall Tax

With global oil and gas companies estimated to be pocketing around $30 million in extra profit every hour as a result of the Iran-war energy shock, campaigners and opposition politicians are intensifying calls on the government to expand the UK's windfall tax. Current legislation, extended under Labour in 2024 to run until March 2030 at a 38% rate, has been criticised as insufficient given the scale of the windfall. Campaign groups are pushing for the proceeds to be used to fund household energy support and to accelerate green investment. Ministers are facing a difficult balance, with revenue from North Sea production also providing income to the Treasury at a time when public finances are under strain.

### 5. MPs Reject Lords-Backed Social Media Ban for Under-16s — Again

The House of Commons this week rejected for a second time a Lords amendment to the Children's Wellbeing and Schools Bill that would have imposed an immediate ban on social media for under-16s. Education minister Olivia Bailey told the Commons that the government prefers a broader national consultation — closing 26 May — over a narrow legislative ban, citing the need to examine evidence from international examples including Australia's approach. Ofsted will now check school mobile phone policy on every inspection, and schools are expected to operate as "phone-free by default". The bill remains in parliamentary "ping pong" between the two chambers, with the Lords likely to press the matter again at its next reading.

### 6. House of Lords Debates Grenfell Tower Memorial Bill

The Grenfell Tower Memorial (Expenditure) Bill passed its second reading in the House of Lords on 14 April. The government bill would authorise public spending to fund a permanent memorial to the 72 victims of the 2017 fire, alongside an archive, an exhibition and a second site to house structural elements of the original tower. Peers from across the chamber expressed broad support for the principle of the bill, though several raised questions about governance, the timeline for completion and ensuring survivors and bereaved families retain a central voice in decisions about the memorial's form and location. The bill is expected to progress through committee stage in the weeks ahead.

### 7. English Local Elections Set for 7 May

England's local elections are now less than three weeks away, with 5,014 council seats up for grabs across 136 local authorities, including all London boroughs. More than 29 councils have had their votes delayed as part of the government's local government reorganisation, leaving around 4.5 million residents unable to hold their councillors to account at the ballot box this cycle. Analysts expect the results to serve as the sharpest test yet of the national political mood — and in particular whether the surge in support for Reform UK in opinion polls translates into gains on the ground.

### 9. UK Flights Disrupted by Iran-War Jet Fuel Crisis

British holidaymakers and business travellers are facing disruption as airlines operating out of UK airports continue to adjust routes and capacity in response to the Iran-war-triggered jet fuel crisis. Several European carriers have already cancelled long-haul routes, and one major European airport group warned this week that the industry faces a "systemic jet fuel shortage" if shipping through the Strait of Hormuz does not normalise by the end of April. UK aviation industry bodies have urged the government to convene an emergency summit with fuel suppliers and airports to manage supply chains, while consumer groups are advising travellers to check policies on refunds and rebooking.

### 10. Ticketmaster Ruled a Monopoly by US Jury — UK Fans Take Note
