### 1. US Naval Blockade of Iran Tightens as Oil Prices Spike

A US military blockade of Iran entered its third day on Wednesday, following the collapse of peace talks in Islamabad on 11–12 April. The blockade, announced by President Trump after ceasefire negotiations ended without agreement, encompasses "the entirety of the Iranian coastline", with CENTCOM warning that ships entering or departing Iranian ports without authorisation are "subject to interception, diversion, and capture." Trump has since vowed to sink any Iranian vessel that approaches the blockade line. Oil prices have surged dramatically in response, with Brent crude hovering near $102 a barrel — up roughly 45% from pre-conflict levels of around $70. On 14 April, Trump hinted that talks could resume within 48 hours, though no new negotiations have been confirmed. A coalition of over 40 nations, led by the UK, is separately planning efforts to restore freedom of navigation through the strait.

### 3. Orbán Era Ends as Hungary's Péter Magyar Wins Landslide Election

Hungary's parliamentary elections on 12 April delivered a historic upset, with Péter Magyar's centre-right Tisza Party ending Viktor Orbán's 16-year grip on power in a landslide. With nearly all precincts counted, Tisza secured approximately 53.6% of the vote and 138 seats in the 199-seat National Assembly — enough for a two-thirds supermajority that allows constitutional amendments. Orbán's Fidesz took just 55 seats with 37.8% of the vote, and Orbán conceded defeat on election night. Voter turnout exceeded 77%, a post-Communist record. The result was widely described as the most significant election in the European Union in 2026, signalling a potential reorientation of Hungary away from its authoritarian trajectory and closer alignment with the EU and liberal democratic norms.

### 4. Peru Presidential Election Heads to Runoff Amid Ballot Chaos

Peru's presidential election, held on 12–13 April, descended into logistical disarray after the failure to deliver ballots to voting centres across the country forced authorities to extend voting by a day. With counting ongoing, no candidate came close to winning outright among the 35 contenders. Conservative Keiko Fujimori led early counts with around 17% of the vote, with ultraconservative Rafael López Aliaga close behind. A runoff is now expected on 7 June, though the two finalists had not been confirmed as of Tuesday. The next president will be Peru's ninth in a decade, inheriting a country gripped by surging violent crime, widespread corruption, and deep voter disillusionment.

### 5. Deadly Stampede at Haiti's Citadelle Laferrière Kills at Least 30

At least 30 people were killed and dozens more injured in a stampede at the Citadelle Laferrière, a UNESCO World Heritage mountaintop fortress in northern Haiti, on 12 April. The tragedy occurred when a local DJ promoted a youth event at the site via TikTok, drawing far more visitors than the historic site could manage. Authorities attributed the deaths to severe overcrowding and failures in crowd management, with rain compounding the disaster at the fortress's entrance. Seven people have since been arrested in connection with the incident, including five police officers. The Citadelle, built in the early 19th century as a defence against a French reinvasion that never came, is one of Haiti's most important cultural symbols.

### 1. UK Refuses to Join US Hormuz Blockade, Leads Coalition

Prime Minister Sir Keir Starmer confirmed that the United Kingdom will not participate in the US naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, describing it as contrary to Britain's commitment to freedom of navigation. Instead, the UK is leading a coalition of more than 40 nations focused on peacefully reopening the critical waterway. Public polling indicates significant debate within Britain about Starmer's approach to the wider US-Iran conflict.

### 2. Iran War Threatens UK Living Standards, Warns Think Tank

The Resolution Foundation warned this week that a typical UK household faces losing nearly £500 in real income this financial year as the Iran war drives energy prices sharply higher. The forecast projects a 0.6% drop in median working-age household income, erasing much of the modest gains anticipated for 2026. Bloomberg separately reported that Britain faces a "lost year for living standards" as a result of the conflict's economic spillover.

### 3. Starmer Pursues Closer EU Ties, Sparking Brexit Row

Keir Starmer has reignited the Brexit debate by announcing plans to seek "dynamic alignment" with EU regulations across food standards, carbon emissions, and electricity markets — meaning the UK would automatically follow new EU rules over time in those areas. The move, to be formalised via a bill at the King's Speech on 13 May, is intended to bolster Britain's stagnant economy. Critics including Conservative shadow minister Andrew Griffith accused the government of reducing Parliament to "a spectator while Brussels sets the terms." Reform UK leader Nigel Farage has also condemned the proposal, calling it a Brexit betrayal.

### 9. Electric Vehicles Hit With Road Tax for First Time

Electric vehicle owners are now paying Vehicle Excise Duty (road tax) for the first time, following changes that took effect earlier this month. EVs previously enjoyed a full exemption, but under the new rules they are subject to the same standard rates as petrol and diesel cars. The change has drawn criticism from EV advocates, who argue it undermines the incentive to switch to cleaner vehicles at a critical moment for the transition.

### 10. Floating Wetlands Proposed to Restore Degraded UK Coastlines

Researchers and conservation groups are promoting the use of floating constructed wetlands as a scalable tool to restore biodiversity along Britain's degraded coastal communities. The approach involves installing buoyant platforms planted with native wetland species in estuaries and coastal inlets, where natural habitats have been lost to development and erosion. Pilots in several locations have shown promising results, and advocates say the method could be deployed widely at relatively low cost.
