### 1. US Naval Blockade of the Strait of Hormuz Begins

The United States military has begun blockading all maritime traffic entering and exiting Iranian ports, after peace talks in Islamabad broke down over the weekend. Negotiations lasted 21 hours before collapsing on the nuclear question — President Trump posting on Truth Social that "most points were agreed to, but the only point that really mattered, NUCLEAR, was not." Trump subsequently threatened to sink Iranian fast-attack ships that approach the blockade. Iran's Revolutionary Guard Corps has vowed retaliation, while the price of Brent crude has surged more than 31% since the war began in late February, trading above $95 a barrel. Major US allies — including France, Germany, Spain, and Turkey — have condemned the blockade, and China has also registered opposition.

### 3. Israel Assaults Hezbollah Stronghold of Bint Jbeil in Southern Lebanon

Fierce fighting erupted on Monday in the strategic southern Lebanese town of Bint Jbeil, a historic Hezbollah stronghold, as the Israeli Defence Forces announced they had completed the town's encirclement and begun a direct assault. The IDF said more than 100 Hezbollah fighters had been killed in the area, and that it expects to achieve full operational control within days. Hezbollah has reported repeated targeting of Israeli forces and vehicles in fierce clashes. The town carries deep symbolic weight — it was from Bint Jbeil's stadium that the late Hezbollah leader Hassan Nasrallah delivered his famous "Liberation" speech following Israel's withdrawal in 2000. Israeli and Lebanese officials are due to hold peace talks in Washington on Tuesday. Lebanon's Health Ministry reports at least 2,055 people killed and more than 6,500 wounded in Israeli strikes across the country.

### 4. Western Allies Reject Trump's Hormuz Blockade; Diplomatic Rift Widens

The US naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz has triggered a significant rupture within the Western alliance. UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer and French President Emmanuel Macron both publicly rejected participation, with Starmer declaring the UK "is not getting dragged in" to the US-Israel war on Iran. France and the UK will co-host a virtual summit of around 30 nations this week to coordinate a multilateral plan to protect shipping in the strait once the conflict ends. Germany, Spain, and Turkey also signalled non-participation. The escalating standoff over one of the world's most critical shipping lanes — through which around 20% of global oil supply passes — has exposed growing fissures between Washington and its traditional allies over the conduct of the conflict.

### 5. Global Economy Rattled by Iran War Energy Shock

Six weeks into the US-Israel war on Iran, energy markets remain severely disrupted. Brent crude peaked at $120 a barrel during the most intense fighting and remains elevated, while UK diesel prices are up around 13% at the pump. Iranian threats and attacks against shipping through the Strait of Hormuz have caused a sharp drop in traffic through the waterway, constraining Persian Gulf oil and gas exports. Analysts have raised their 2026 Brent crude forecasts by around 30%. The OECD has warned the conflict will weigh on global and UK economic growth, with the UK's forecast cut to 1.1% GDP growth for the year. Higher energy costs are expected to push UK inflation higher and increase household gas bills later in 2026.

## Section 2: UK News Overview

### 1. Starmer Rules Out Joining US Hormuz Blockade

Prime Minister Keir Starmer confirmed on Monday that the United Kingdom will not participate in the US-declared naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz. Speaking to BBC Radio, Starmer said: "We are not supporting the blockade," and stressed that Britain's priority is getting the strait "open and fully open." Instead, the UK is working with France and other allies on a coordinated multinational plan to protect commercial shipping once the conflict de-escalates. The move signals a notable divergence from Washington, though the government insists it remains committed to the broader alliance.

### 3. Lords Debates Grenfell Tower Memorial Bill

The House of Lords debated the Grenfell Tower Memorial (Expenditure) Bill at second reading this week. The legislation would authorise public funding for a permanent memorial to the 72 victims of the 2017 Grenfell Tower fire, as well as associated archives, an exhibition space, and a second site where structural elements of the tower could be laid to rest. As a money bill, the Lords cannot amend it, and subsequent stages were handled formally on the same day. The bill received cross-party support in the Commons with no amendments tabled.

### 4. First Charge Under New Channel Crossing Law After Migrants Die

British prosecutors have brought the first charges under the Border Security, Asylum and Immigration Act, following the deaths of four migrants attempting to cross the English Channel on 9 April. Alnour Mohamed Ali, a 27-year-old Sudanese national, appeared at Folkestone Magistrates' Court on Saturday, accused of endangering life at sea by piloting an overcrowded inflatable in strong currents without adequate safety equipment. The new law extends UK criminal jurisdiction to small-boat pilots even when incidents occur in French waters, with convictions carrying potential life sentences. As of this month, a total of 197,746 migrants have crossed the Channel in small boats since 2018.

### 5. UK Spring Forecast: Borrowing at Six-Year Low, But Growth Revised Down

The Office for Budget Responsibility's Spring 2026 Forecast, delivered by Chancellor Rachel Reeves in early March, continues to shape the economic debate. UK borrowing is set to be the lowest in six years and below the G7 average for the first time in 22 years. However, GDP growth for 2026 has been revised down to 1.1% from the earlier forecast of 1.4%, partly due to the Iran war's impact on energy costs. Inflation is expected to return to the 2% target in late 2026, while unemployment is forecast to rise modestly to around 5.3%. The OBR projected that workers will be over £1,000 a year better off in real terms, though the cost of living pressures from rising fuel prices threaten to offset those gains.
