## Section 1: Top World News Stories

### 1. US Naval Blockade of the Strait of Hormuz Takes Effect

The United States military's naval blockade of Iranian ports came into force at 10 a.m. ET on Monday, after weekend peace talks in Islamabad collapsed over Iran's refusal to abandon its nuclear programme. President Trump announced the blockade via social media, writing that "IRAN IS UNWILLING TO GIVE UP ITS NUCLEAR AMBITIONS!" CENTCOM clarified that the blockade targets vessels entering or leaving Iranian ports and coastal waters, but will not impede passage through the strait to non-Iranian ports. At least one Iranian oil tanker was reported to have defied the order and passed through the Strait of Hormuz. Oil prices surged above $100 a barrel in response, with global energy and food supply chains already under severe strain — Iran's closure of the strait since late February had blocked around 10 million barrels per day of oil production from reaching markets. The UN Secretary-General called for immediate de-escalation.

### 2. Hungary Ends Orbán Era as Peter Magyar Wins Landslide Election

In one of the most significant European political upsets in years, Hungarian Prime Minister Viktor Orbán conceded defeat on Sunday after 16 years in power. Peter Magyar's centre-right Tisza party won 53.6% of the vote and secured 138 of 199 parliamentary seats — a two-thirds supermajority. Voter turnout hit nearly 80%, the highest since the 1990s. Orbán's Fidesz party received 37.8% and 55 seats. Magyar, a former insider who broke with the ruling establishment over corruption allegations, told jubilant supporters that the victory was "visible from the moon and every window in Hungary." The result is expected to end Hungary's long-standing obstruction within the European Union, potentially unblocking a €90 billion ($105bn) loan package for Ukraine that Orbán had repeatedly vetoed.

### 3. Pope Leo XIV Defies Trump on Iran War, Begins 11-Day Africa Tour

Pope Leo XIV began an 11-day apostolic journey to Africa on Monday, landing in Algiers on the first-ever papal visit to Algeria — but the trip's opening hours were dominated by a remarkable public clash with President Trump. Trump had posted overnight that Pope Leo was "not doing a good job" and should "stop catering to the Radical Left," after the pope publicly appealed for peace and called the US-Israel war on Iran a demonstration of the "delusion of omnipotence." Speaking to reporters on his flight to Algiers, Leo — the first US-born pope in history — said he had "no fear of the Trump administration" and pledged to "continue to speak out strongly against war." In Algeria, he called for an end to "neocolonial tendencies" in world affairs and urged dialogue to resolve the Iran conflict. The tour will also take the pope to Cameroon, Angola, and Equatorial Guinea.

### 4. Israel Intensifies Lebanon Offensive; Infant Killed at Funeral

Israeli forces continued to intensify their ground offensive in southern Lebanon on Monday, cutting off all main roads leading to the besieged town of Bint Jbeil while pounding it with air strikes, artillery, and — according to Lebanese security sources — white phosphorus munitions. An Israeli air strike killed an infant girl during her own father's funeral, prompting international condemnation. Lebanon's Health Ministry reports more than 2,000 people killed and over 6,500 wounded since Israel launched its ground invasion in early March, with more than one million people — around 20% of Lebanon's population — displaced. Hezbollah has rejected ceasefire overtures from Israel, stating that any truce must include an Israeli commitment to end its war with Iran. Israeli and Lebanese officials are due to hold negotiations in Washington on Tuesday.

### 5. Western Allies Fracture Over Iran War Strategy

Monday's blockade announcement deepened a growing rift within the Western alliance. UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer, French President Emmanuel Macron, Germany's Chancellor and the governments of Spain and Turkey all publicly rejected participation in the US naval blockade. Starmer told BBC Radio: "We are not supporting the blockade — the UK is not getting dragged into this war." France and the UK are co-hosting a virtual summit of around 30 nations this week to explore a multilateral framework to protect international shipping in the strait. China also opposed the blockade, warning of "serious consequences" for global trade. The diplomatic fragmentation raises questions about NATO cohesion and the durability of US-led alliances in an era of contested foreign policy choices.

### 2. Iran War Costs UK Households an Estimated £500 a Year

Bloomberg analysis published over the weekend estimates that a typical UK household will be nearly £500 ($672) worse off due to surging energy prices driven by the Iran war. Diesel prices are up roughly 13% at the pump since the conflict began in late February, and gas bills are forecast to rise further later in 2026 as the oil price shock works its way through supply chains. Starmer said he was "fed up" with households bearing the cost of geopolitical instability beyond Britain's control.

### 3. UK Growth Forecast Cut to 1.1% by OBR; Inflation Expected to Rise

The Office for Budget Responsibility has lowered its 2026 GDP growth forecast from 1.4% to 1.1%, citing weaker-than-expected data at end of 2025, subdued business sentiment, and the impact of the Iran war on energy and food prices. Inflation — which stood at 3% in February — is now expected to rise to 3.5% rather than fall towards the 2% target, with the Bank of England holding its base rate at 3.75%. Mortgage lenders have responded by pushing fixed rates higher, with the average two-year fix now at 5.84%.

### 6. Grenfell Memorial Bill Passes Lords Second Reading

The Grenfell Tower Memorial (Expenditure) Bill passed its second reading in the House of Lords this week with cross-party support. The legislation authorises public spending on a permanent memorial to the 72 people who died in the June 2017 fire, including archive, exhibition, and structural commemoration facilities. As a money bill, the Lords cannot amend it; remaining stages were taken formally. The bill is expected to receive Royal Assent in the coming weeks.

### 8. Immigration Reforms: Higher English Bar and Longer Settlement Route

New UK immigration rules taking effect in April 2026 raise the English proficiency requirement for settlement (Indefinite Leave to Remain) applications from B1 to B2, while extending the qualifying period for most sponsored work routes from five to ten years. The Immigration Salary List will be replaced by a narrower Temporary Shortage List. Critics, including NHS Employers, have warned that tighter visa rules risk worsening staffing shortages in health and care sectors.
