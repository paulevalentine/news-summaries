### 1. Hungary's Political Earthquake: Magyar Defeats Orbán After 16 Years

Péter Magyar's centre-right Tisza Party has won Hungary's parliamentary election in a landslide, ending Viktor Orbán's 16-year grip on power in one of the most significant political shifts in modern European history. With nearly all precincts counted, Magyar's party secured 138 of 199 parliamentary seats on 53.6% of the vote, while Orbán's Fidesz took just 55 seats on 37.8%. Voter turnout was the highest since the 1990s. Orbán conceded, calling the result "painful but clear." Magyar told supporters they had "liberated Hungary," and the result was warmly received in Brussels — European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen declared: "Hungary has chosen Europe." The new government is expected to take a markedly more pro-EU stance, reversing years of friction with Brussels over democratic backsliding and rule-of-law concerns.

### 2. US Imposes Blockade on Iranian Ports as Peace Talks Collapse

The United States began a military blockade of Iranian port traffic in the Strait of Hormuz on Monday, after peace talks between the US and Iran collapsed without agreement. President Trump announced the move, vowing to interdict vessels that had paid what he called an "illegal toll" to Iran and warning that US forces would sink any Iranian ships approaching the blockade line. CENTCOM clarified that the action targets ships entering or leaving Iranian ports and coastal areas, and does not impede general freedom of navigation through the strait. Oil prices surged sharply in response, with Brent crude rising around 7% to hover at approximately $102 per barrel — compared to roughly $70 before the conflict began. Allies including the UK and France declined to participate, with London instead working to build a coalition to protect freedom of navigation.

### 4. Haiti Fortress Stampede Kills At Least 30

A deadly stampede at the historic Citadelle Laferrière — a UNESCO World Heritage site and mountaintop fortress in northern Haiti near the town of Milot — has killed at least 30 people and injured dozens more. The tragedy unfolded during a school outing and cultural celebration when severe overcrowding near the fortress entrance prompted a rush to exit, reportedly exacerbated by heavy rainfall. Authorities cited a significant failure in crowd management. The Citadelle, built in the early nineteenth century following Haiti's independence, is one of the country's most visited tourist landmarks. Haiti's government has launched an investigation into the disaster.

### 5. Peru Election Thrown Into Chaos by Logistical Failures

Peru's presidential election has descended into uncertainty after logistical failures — including missing ballot boxes, absent voting materials, and incomplete ballot delivery — prevented approximately 63,000 registered voters from casting their votes on election day, Sunday 12 April. Authorities extended voting for a further day for affected residents in Lima and overseas polling stations. Official results showed no candidate close to the 50% threshold required to avoid a runoff, with Keiko Fujimori on approximately 16.9% and Rafael López Aliaga on 13.9% among the largest tallies. A second round, likely in June, now appears virtually certain. The chaotic election is Peru's latest political crisis — the country has had nine presidents in under a decade.

## Section 2: UK News — Domestic Overview

### 1. Southport Inquiry Finds Attack Was Preventable

The Phase 1 report of the Southport Inquiry, published on 13 April, concluded that the 2024 knife attack which killed three young girls — Elsie Dot Stancombe, Alice da Silva Aguiar, and Bebe King — at a Taylor Swift-themed children's dance class was "foreseeable and avoidable." The report identifies 67 recommendations and five major areas of systemic failure across the perpetrator's family, police, and social services. A pivotal missed opportunity occurred in March 2022 when the attacker told officers on a bus that he wanted to stab someone, but was not arrested due to officers' lack of knowledge of his risk history. The murder weapon — a kitchen knife — was purchased from Amazon using a VPN and his father's account details. Prime Minister Keir Starmer called the findings "harrowing" and pledged "fundamental changes."

Prime Minister Keir Starmer confirmed that the UK will not participate in the US military blockade of the Strait of Hormuz. The government said it is "urgently working with France and other partners to put together a wide coalition to protect freedom of navigation" through the strategically vital waterway. The decision reflects both the UK's diplomatic distancing from Washington's escalatory posture and the economic stakes involved: the Iran war has already pushed oil prices past $100 per barrel, rattling UK household budgets and financial markets.

### 6. NHS Survival Rates Ranked Near Bottom Among Developed Nations

A major international analysis has found that UK patient survival rates are among the weakest in the developed world, with Britain ranking near the bottom of 22 comparable countries for treatable mortality — with only the United States performing worse. The report highlights chronic underinvestment: the UK has around 19 MRI, CT, and PET scanners per million people, compared to roughly 50 in similar tax-funded systems, and hospital bed capacity is lower than in many comparable countries. NHS performance and waiting list reduction remain among the top political priorities for the government and opposition alike.

### 7. National Living Wage Rises to £12.71 per Hour

From April 2026, the National Living Wage increased by 4.1% to £12.71 per hour for workers aged 21 and over, benefiting over a million of the lowest-paid workers in the UK. Workers aged 18–20 saw an even steeper increase of 8.5% to their minimum wage rate. The government says the rises are part of its commitment to improving living standards, though some business groups have warned of added cost pressures, particularly for small employers already contending with rising energy bills and broader economic uncertainty.
