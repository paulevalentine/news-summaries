## 1. World News: Top Stories

### US-Iran Crisis Deepens as Trump Orders Naval Blockade of Strait of Hormuz

After peace talks between the United States and Iran collapsed at the weekend in Islamabad, President Donald Trump announced on Sunday that the US military would impose a naval blockade on Iranian ports in the Strait of Hormuz — one of the world's most critical oil shipping lanes. The blockade came into effect on Monday, with US CENTCOM clarifying that it targets vessels entering or departing Iranian ports rather than all shipping through the strait. Oil prices surged by around 8%, with Brent crude reaching $104 a barrel. Several US allies, including the UK, France, Spain and Turkey, condemned the move and declined to participate. Analysts have warned the blockade risks being interpreted by Iran as an act of war.

### Hungary: Péter Magyar Defeats Orbán in Landslide, Ending 16-Year Rule

In a historic result, opposition leader Péter Magyar and his centre-right Tisza Party won Sunday's Hungarian parliamentary election in a landslide, ending Viktor Orbán's 16-year grip on power. With nearly all votes counted, Tisza secured around 54% of the vote and 138 of 199 parliamentary seats — a supermajority that will allow the new government to amend the constitution. Orbán's Fidesz party received approximately 38% and 55 seats. Magyar, 45, a former Orbán loyalist who turned opposition figurehead in 2024, has pledged to rebuild Hungary's ties with the EU and NATO, restore democratic checks and balances, and root out corruption.

### At Least 25 Killed in Stampede at Haiti's Citadelle Laferrière

A deadly stampede at Haiti's Citadelle Laferrière — a mountaintop UNESCO World Heritage fortress in the country's north — has killed at least 25 to 30 people and injured dozens more. The tragedy occurred on Saturday when overcrowding, partly attributed to a large social-media-promoted event aimed at young people, led to a fatal crowd surge. Haitian authorities have arrested seven people in connection with the disaster, including five police officers. The Citadelle, built in the early 19th century, is one of Haiti's most visited tourist sites and a symbol of national identity.

### Peru Presidential Election Headed for June Runoff After Logistical Chaos

Peru's presidential election, held on Sunday 12 April, descended into logistical chaos after ballot papers, boxes and voting materials failed to reach tens of thousands of polling stations. Electoral authorities extended voting by a day for affected voters. Early partial results pointed to a runoff between right-wing candidates, with Keiko Fujimori — daughter of disgraced former President Alberto Fujimori — leading with around 17% of the preliminary vote. A June runoff is now considered virtually certain, as no candidate is expected to cross the 50% threshold needed to avoid a second round. The election is Peru's ninth change of president in less than a decade.

## 2. UK News: Overview

### 1. Starmer Rejects US Blockade, Leads International Push to Reopen Strait

Prime Minister Keir Starmer confirmed on Monday that the UK will not participate in the US naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz. Speaking to the BBC, Starmer said "we are not supporting the blockade" and that Britain is "not getting dragged in" to the conflict. Instead, he announced that the UK had convened more than 40 nations committed to restoring freedom of navigation, and that the UK and France would co-host a multilateral summit in the coming days to coordinate that effort. The stance puts the UK at odds with Washington but in alignment with other European allies.

### 2. Southport Inquiry: 2024 Knife Attack on Girls Was Preventable

The Phase 1 report of the Southport Public Inquiry was published on Monday, concluding that the 2024 knife attack at a Taylor Swift-themed children's dance class — which killed Bebe King (6), Elsie Dot Stancombe (7) and Alice da Silva Aguiar (9), and injured ten others — was foreseeable and could have been prevented. The report identified five areas of systemic failure, including the absence of clear risk ownership, failures by multiple agencies to share information, a misunderstanding of the perpetrator's autism diagnosis, and significant parental failures. The inquiry makes 67 recommendations. Phase 2 is expected to report in Spring 2027.

### 4. NHS Waiting Lists: Progress Made But Backlogs Remain Severe

The NHS waiting list in England stood at approximately 7.31 million cases as of the latest figures, representing around 6.19 million individual patients. While the list has fallen from its peak — reaching its lowest level in nearly three years earlier in the year — the diagnostic waiting list of 1.68 million includes 19.9% of patients waiting longer than six weeks, far above the target of less than 1%. NHS activity is rising but not yet fast enough to clear the accumulated backlog, and the Health Foundation's assessment one year into the government's reforms gives a mixed picture.

### 5. Energy Bills: April Price Cap Falls but Uncertainty Looms

The Ofgem energy price cap fell by approximately 7% from 1 April 2026, reducing the average household's annual bill by around £117 to a cap equivalent of £1,641. An additional £150 Warm Home Discount will benefit around 6 million households. However, energy analysts are warning that the ongoing conflict in the Middle East and the Strait of Hormuz crisis could push global gas prices upward again when the cap is reviewed in July, potentially reversing some of the recent gains.

### 6. US Tariffs Weighing on UK Economic Growth

The ongoing effect of US trade tariffs — including a 10% baseline tariff on most UK goods, plus 25% tariffs on steel, aluminium and vehicles — is cutting into UK economic growth. Oxford Economics has forecast UK GDP growth of just below 1% for this year, down from a previous estimate of 1.5%. The Hormuz crisis and associated oil price spike add further uncertainty to the outlook. The UK-US trade deal agreed in May 2025 provides some mitigation, but significant tariff burdens remain, particularly on UK manufacturers.

### 7. Channel Crossings Continue; New Asylum Rules Take Effect

Small boat crossings of the English Channel continue, with four people dying in a crossing attempt on 9 April 2026. New asylum rules that came into force in March 2026 mean claimants will receive "core protection" status for 2.5 years rather than indefinite leave to remain, requiring repeated reviews over a 20-year period. Immigration and nationality application fees also rose from 8 April. Immigration detention has risen to nearly 23,000 people. The government is also restricting asylum seekers' right to work to degree-level occupations after one year of waiting.
