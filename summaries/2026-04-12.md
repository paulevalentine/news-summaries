## World News

### 1. US–Iran Peace Talks Collapse in Islamabad

High-stakes diplomatic negotiations between the United States and Iran broke down on Sunday after 21 hours of marathon talks in Islamabad, Pakistan. US Vice President JD Vance, leading a delegation of nearly 300 officials including special envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner, said Tehran had refused to accept Washington's core demand: a "fundamental commitment" not to develop nuclear weapons. Iran's delegation, led by parliamentary speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf and Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi, rejected the terms. The talks had been convened following a fragile two-week ceasefire brokered by Pakistan after weeks of US–Israeli strikes on Iran beginning in late February. With the talks now failed, President Trump threatened to impose a naval blockade of ships in the Strait of Hormuz if Iran does not reopen the waterway. The collapse has deepened fears of an escalating conflict with severe consequences for global trade and energy supply.

### 2. Hungary Goes to the Polls — Orbán Faces Historic Challenge

Hungarians voted on Sunday in a parliamentary election that could end Viktor Orbán's 16-year grip on power. The opposition Tisza party, led by former Fidesz insider Péter Magyar, entered polling day with a significant lead, with surveys from Medián showing Tisza on 58% against Fidesz on 33%. Magyar has built his campaign on an anti-corruption platform, accusing Orbán and his allies of personal enrichment while ordinary Hungarians have grown poorer. Hungary is the only EU member state currently ranked "partly free" by Freedom House. The result carries implications well beyond Hungary's borders: an Orbán defeat would represent a significant shift in European politics and potentially alter Hungary's relationship with both Brussels and Moscow.

### 3. Artemis II Crew Safely Returns from Historic Lunar Mission

NASA's Artemis II crew splashed down in the Pacific Ocean off the coast of San Diego on 10 April, completing a nine-day mission that marked humanity's first crewed journey beyond low Earth orbit since Apollo 17 in 1972. Commander Reid Wiseman, pilot Victor Glover, and mission specialist Christina Koch (all NASA), alongside Canadian Space Agency astronaut Jeremy Hansen, flew around the Moon before returning safely to Earth. The crew reached a record distance of 252,756 miles (406,771 km) from Earth, surpassing Apollo 13's previous record. Recovery teams aboard the USS Murtha extracted the crew and flew them to NASA's Johnson Space Center in Houston. The mission is a major milestone in NASA's broader Artemis programme to return humans to the lunar surface.

### 4. Peru Holds Presidential Election Amid Deep Political Crisis

Voters in Peru went to the polls on Sunday to elect the country's ninth president in less than a decade, with 35 candidates on the ballot reflecting the extreme fragmentation of Peruvian politics. The election takes place against a backdrop of rising corruption scandals, escalating gang violence, and public disillusionment with the political class. No single candidate is expected to secure an outright majority, making a run-off likely. International observers have raised concerns about the integrity of the process, and many Peruvians remain sceptical about whether any new government can break the cycle of instability. The result will be closely watched by Latin American neighbours and foreign investors concerned about the country's direction.

### 5. China–Taiwan Tensions After Xi–KMT Meeting

Chinese President Xi Jinping met with Cheng Li-wun, a senior figure in Taiwan's Kuomintang (KMT) opposition party, with both sides announcing agreement to "pursue cross-strait peace." Taiwan's ruling Democratic Progressive Party (DPP) immediately condemned the meeting, warning it gives Beijing a vehicle to apply pressure on Taiwanese democracy through the KMT and undermines the island's sovereignty. The meeting has inflamed domestic political debate on Taiwan and drawn cautious concern from Western governments. Washington has so far not commented formally, though officials are reported to be monitoring the situation closely.

## UK News

### 1. Iran War Sends UK Energy Bills Soaring

The ongoing US–Israeli war on Iran and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz have sent British energy costs surging, with petrol prices up 14p per litre and diesel up 29p per litre since late February. UK wholesale natural gas prices have risen roughly 75% over the same period. Energy analysts Cornwall Insight now forecast that the household energy price cap will jump by approximately 20% from July — rising from £1,641 to around £1,973 per year for a typical customer. The government is weighing emergency support measures, with one option being to inject extra funds into the Crisis and Resilience Fund, a £1 billion-per-year council-run scheme, to help the most vulnerable families.

### 2. Fuel Thefts Surge Across UK Forecourts

A sharp rise in petrol prices linked to the Iran war has triggered an unprecedented wave of drive-off theft at UK filling stations. Reported damage at around 500 forecourts increased by 27% in March alone. Incidents span the social spectrum — Sunday newspapers report that even owners of luxury vehicles including Ferraris and Aston Martins have been caught stealing fuel. Filling station operators and the Petrol Retailers Association have called on the government to fast-track prepayment legislation already under consideration, while police forces have warned that theft-related crime is placing increasing strain on resources.

### 3. Defence Investment Plan Delays Raise Alarm

Insiders at the Ministry of Defence have warned that alarming delays to the government's Defence Investment Plan are undermining Britain's military readiness. The plan — promised in autumn 2025 to outline how a substantial increase in defence spending would be delivered — has still not been published, and sources suggest a significant funding gap exists between programme costs and money committed in the Spring Spending Review. Prime Minister Keir Starmer has pledged to raise core defence spending to 3.5% of GDP by 2035, with a further 1.5% for security and resilience, but critics warn that without a credible delivery plan the targets are unenforceable. The *i Weekend* newspaper led with warnings on Sunday that the delays risk leaving the UK ill-prepared.

### 4. Migrants Using Ukrainian Refugee Scheme to Enter UK

Approximately 3,500 migrants from 112 countries outside Ukraine have entered Britain using the Homes for Ukraine humanitarian scheme since its launch, according to a report in *The Sunday Telegraph*. The scheme, which was created to offer temporary refuge to those fleeing Russia's invasion, has been exploited by nationals of non-conflict countries who falsely claim Ukrainian status or use document fraud. The Home Office said it is reviewing the scheme's eligibility criteria, but critics argue enforcement is too weak and called for immediate suspension of applications while audits are carried out. Refugee charities have urged proportionate responses that do not penalise genuine Ukrainian refugees.

### 5. Lloyds Bank Appoints AI System to Its Board

Lloyds Banking Group has become one of the first major UK financial institutions to formally appoint an artificial intelligence system to its board of directors, according to *The Sunday Times*. The AI is designed to analyse large volumes of financial data, flag emerging risks, and contribute to strategic decision-making processes alongside human board members. The move has been welcomed by some as a step towards better risk governance, but has drawn criticism from shareholder advocates who argue it blurs accountability. The Financial Conduct Authority has indicated it is monitoring the development closely and may issue guidance on AI board representation later this year.

### 6. UK Economy Slowing — Recession Fears Persist

The Office for Budget Responsibility (OBR) has downgraded its 2026 GDP growth forecast to 1.1%, down from an earlier projection of 1.4%, Chancellor Rachel Reeves confirmed at the Spring Statement in March. Unemployment is forecast to peak at 5.3% in 2026 before easing in 2027. Analysis from RSM suggests that the UK's "real economy" — the goods and services-producing sectors — ended 2025 already in contraction, even as headline growth figures remained marginally positive. The energy shock caused by the Iran war has added fresh downward pressure, with economists warning of a potential inflationary surge that could force the Bank of England to reconsider rate cuts.

### 7. Stricter Immigration Rules Take Effect in April

A package of tightened immigration rules came into force this month, including raising English language requirements for skilled worker and settlement visas from B1 to B2 level, and extending the qualifying period for Indefinite Leave to Remain from five to ten years for most sponsored work routes. The Immigration Skills Charge has also increased by 32% to £1,320 per person per year. NHS Employers has raised concerns that the reforms will further constrain the health service's ability to recruit international staff at a time of severe workforce shortages. Immigration lawyers have warned that thousands of applicants already in the pipeline may be affected by the changes.

### 8. NHS Launches Major Structural Overhaul

Twelve NHS Integrated Care Boards (ICBs) in England began merging into six from April 2026, in what the government describes as a cost-saving consolidation of management structures. The reorganisation forms part of a wider drive to cut non-clinical overhead, with the number of such staff barely growing since early 2024 as NHS trusts battle severe financial deficits. Critics, including the British Medical Association, have warned that repeated structural change diverts clinical energy and risks service disruption during a period of already significant pressure on emergency and elective care. The NHS is also dealing with knock-on demand increases from patients concerned about energy bill affordability affecting their health.

### 9. Welfare Reform Sparks Debate Over Disability Benefits

Government plans to reform health-related and disability benefits continue to face strong criticism from anti-poverty campaigners and disability rights groups. While the Universal Credit standard allowance is increasing above CPI inflation for 2026/27 to 2029/30, proposed cuts to Personal Independence Payment (PIP) and work capability assessments are drawing significant opposition. Child Poverty Action Group (CPAG) has warned that the reforms risk pushing thousands of disabled people deeper into poverty at a time when the cost-of-living crisis is already acute. Ministers argue the current system is unsustainable and that reforms are designed to support more people into employment.

### 10. Easter Weekend Travel Disruption

Rail and road networks across the UK experienced significant disruption over the Easter weekend, with engineering works on several major lines and elevated motorway congestion compounding an already difficult period for travellers. AA and RAC reported that Saturday and Sunday ranked among the busiest travel days of the year so far, with fuel-related queues at some service stations adding to delays. Airlines at Heathrow and Manchester flagged above-average load factors, with some short-haul routes delayed due to air traffic control resourcing. Travel groups called on National Highways and Network Rail to better coordinate planned works to avoid future Easter weekend clashes.
