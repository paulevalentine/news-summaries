## World News

### 1. US-Iran Nuclear Talks: Hopes of Breakthrough as Pakistan Mediates

Following the failure of initial direct talks in Islamabad earlier this month, Pakistan is again playing a central role in brokering a nuclear agreement between the United States and Iran. A high-level Pakistani delegation, led by Army Chief General Asim Munir, visited Tehran on Wednesday carrying a message from Washington, and both sides expressed cautious optimism about a potential second round of negotiations. The key sticking points remain Iran's uranium enrichment programme — Washington wants a 20-year moratorium while Iran has offered five years — as well as control of the Strait of Hormuz and war reparations. The White House described the Pakistan-mediated discussions as "productive and ongoing" and said Islamabad would likely host the next round of talks. A two-week ceasefire agreed on 8 April is still holding.

### 2. Russia Launches Massive Drone and Missile Attack on Ukraine

Russia unleashed one of its largest attacks of the year on Ukraine overnight, firing nearly 660 drones and 44 cruise and ballistic missiles in a hours-long bombardment that stretched across the country. At least 16–18 people were killed, including a 12-year-old boy in Kyiv, and more than 100 were wounded. Eight people died in a single residential building in Odesa, which was struck first by a drone and then by a ballistic missile. Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy, who has been touring European allies to appeal for additional air defence support, warned that the ongoing Iran war is diverting critical weapons away from Ukraine. The attack followed a brief Easter ceasefire and came as peace talks remain deadlocked.

### 3. Global Jet Fuel Crisis Deepens as Strait of Hormuz Remains Blocked

Iran's near-closure of the Strait of Hormuz — through which around a fifth of the world's oil flows — has triggered what analysts are describing as the worst energy supply disruption since the 1970s oil crises. Crude oil prices have surged to near $150 per barrel, and jet fuel prices have doubled. European airports are imposing refuelling restrictions and airlines have begun cancelling flights: Scandinavian airline SAS has cancelled 1,000 April flights, while Ryanair's CEO warned of summer capacity cuts. One European airport group issued a stark warning of a "systemic jet fuel shortage" if Hormuz traffic does not normalise by month's end. Asia is considered the hardest-hit region, with some countries rationing fuel and restricting exports.

### 4. Hungary's Orbán Era Ends as Péter Magyar Wins Landslide Election

Hungary's Tisza Party, led by Péter Magyar, won a decisive victory in parliamentary elections on 12 April, ending 16 years of Viktor Orbán's rule. Tisza secured 53.6% of the vote and 138 of 199 parliamentary seats — a two-thirds supermajority sufficient to amend the constitution — while Orbán's Fidesz took 37.8% and just 55 seats. Turnout exceeded 77%, a post-Communist record. Magyar, a former Orbán loyalist who broke with the movement in 2024, is expected to reverse Hungary's adversarial relationship with the EU, unblock €18 billion in frozen EU funds, and unlock a €90 billion EU loan to Ukraine that Orbán had vetoed. EU and NATO leaders broadly welcomed the result.

### 5. Israel Strikes Lebanon as Trump Brokers First Leader-to-Leader Call in Decades

Israeli airstrikes on southern Lebanon killed at least 16 people on Wednesday, with strikes targeting multiple areas in the south of the country. President Trump announced that the leaders of Israel and Lebanon would hold a direct telephone call on Thursday — the first such leader-to-leader contact between the two countries in roughly three decades. The proposed call is seen as a step toward a potential ceasefire arrangement. Trump has been pursuing a broader diplomatic settlement in parallel with his administration's focus on the Iran nuclear negotiations, as regional instability continues to escalate.

## UK News Overview

### 1. Kent Meningitis Outbreak: Cases Rise to 34 as NHS Response Questioned

The meningitis B outbreak centred on Canterbury has now reached 34 confirmed cases, up from 29 earlier in the week, according to the UK Health Security Agency. The cluster is linked to a superspreading event at Club Chemistry, a Canterbury nightclub, between 5–7 March, attended by approximately 4,800 people — predominantly students from the University of Kent and Canterbury Christ Church University. Two people have died. Reports have emerged that NHS England waited two days before raising the alarm about the outbreak, prompting scrutiny of the emergency response timeline. Mass vaccination campaigns for those who attended the venue or had close contact are ongoing.

### 2. UK Launches Social Media Trial for 300 Teenagers

The Department for Science, Innovation and Technology has launched a six-week pilot with 300 teenagers across the UK testing different approaches to restricting social media access. Families are split into four groups: one banning social media entirely at home using parental controls, one capping use at one hour per day, one imposing a 9pm–7am digital curfew, and a control group carrying on as normal. The trial follows Parliament's rejection of a blanket ban on under-16s using social media platforms and forms part of a broader digital wellbeing consultation that has received over 30,000 responses. Researchers will assess the impact on sleep, family life, and schoolwork before and after the six-week period.

### 3. UK Claims World-First in Quantum Computing Rollout

The government has announced a £2 billion investment programme — "ProQure: Scaling UK Quantum Computing" — positioning the UK as the first country in the world to commit to deploying quantum computers at commercial scale, with a target of the early 2030s. Over £500 million is dedicated to quantum computing applications in pharmaceuticals, financial services, and energy, with additional funding for quantum sensing, navigation, and skills infrastructure. A separate £500 million Sovereign AI Fund is also launching this month. The initiative, backed by both the Technology Secretary and Chancellor Rachel Reeves, was framed as central to the government's economic growth and national security strategy.

### 4. Typical Family Now Pays £4,000 More in Tax Under Reeves

A new analysis reported on the front page of the Daily Telegraph concludes that the average UK family is paying around £4,000 more per year in tax since Chancellor Rachel Reeves took office, driven by frozen income tax thresholds, higher council tax, increased employer National Insurance contributions, and other fiscal measures. Reeves' third budget, delivered in March 2026, included £40 billion in tax rises — the largest single-budget tax increase since 1993. A new "mansion tax" surcharge on properties valued above £2 million was also announced, applying from 2028. The findings add political pressure ahead of May's local elections, where Labour is facing significant projected losses.

### 5. Labour Faces Collapse in Local and Welsh Elections

New polling ahead of the 7 May local elections projects Labour losing control in Wales and suffering widespread council losses across England. In Wales, surveys consistently show Labour in third place — behind both Plaid Cymru and Reform UK — in what commentators have described as potentially "cataclysmic," given that Labour has not lost a Welsh election since 1931. Nationally, Labour's support has fallen from around 35% in 2022 to approximately 20%, with Reform making strong inroads in northern metropolitan areas. The results of May's elections could significantly reshape the opposition landscape ahead of the next general election.

### 6. Gibraltar-Spain Border Set to Open in July Under New Treaty

A landmark UK-EU agreement confirming Gibraltar's accession to the Schengen Area will provisionally come into force on 15 July 2026, removing physical border checks between Gibraltar and Spain. The treaty removes the land frontier fence (*la Verja*) at the Gibraltar–La Línea de la Concepción crossing, relocating immigration controls to Gibraltar's airport and seaport under a juxtaposed system involving Spanish and Gibraltarian officers — mirroring the Eurostar border control model. The UK government has confirmed the agreement does not affect British sovereignty over the territory. The deal was welcomed by Gibraltar's government as a historic removal of a post-Brexit trade and travel barrier.

### 7. Teenager First to Face Charges Under New UK Border Legislation

A 16-year-old is due to appear at Margate Magistrates' Court charged under new UK border legislation, believed to be the first person to face prosecution under the new laws. The teenager is accused of endangering others during a Channel sea crossing on 5 January. The prosecution marks the government's first use of criminal charges against individuals crossing the Channel in small boats and is expected to draw scrutiny from legal and human rights organisations who have questioned the proportionality of applying criminal penalties to a minor. The case will be closely watched as a test of the new legislation's application in practice.

### 9. Treasury Committee Scrutinises Bank of England Deputy Governor Nominee

The House of Commons Treasury Committee held an appointment hearing for Katharine Braddick, the government's nominee to become Deputy Governor for Prudential Regulation at the Bank of England and Chief Executive of the Prudential Regulation Authority. The hearing comes as the UK faces rising inflation pressures linked to the global energy crisis: oil near $150 per barrel is feeding through to higher petrol, energy, and food prices, complicating the Bank's monetary policy outlook. Braddick's appointment requires parliamentary endorsement before formal confirmation and will be subject to a vote of the committee.

### 10. UK Energy Costs Under Fresh Pressure as Global Fuel Crisis Bites

British households and businesses are facing renewed energy cost pressures as the Iran-driven Strait of Hormuz crisis pushes oil to near-record levels. Petrol prices at UK forecourts have risen sharply in recent weeks, and energy analysts warn that if the blockade persists into summer, domestic energy bills could face another upward revision in the autumn price cap review. UK airlines are already passing higher jet fuel costs on to passengers through fuel surcharges and higher fares. The government has not yet announced emergency energy support measures, though pressure is growing from consumer groups and opposition parties to act ahead of a potential summer crunch.
